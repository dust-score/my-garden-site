## [[2. Main Theoretical Pillars/The Messianic Trap#^messiah\|Messianism]]

**Messianism** is the expectation of a redeemer—a figure, event, or transformation—that will bring history to its fulfilment. The term derives from the Hebrew *mashiach* ("anointed one"), used in the Hebrew Bible for priests and kings consecrated to a sacred task.[^1] In its developed Jewish forms, messianic expectation oscillates between a restorative and a utopian pole: the return to an idealised past (the Davidic kingdom) or the arrival of a condition that has never yet existed.[^2]

For **Gershom Scholem**, these two tendencies—restorative and utopian—constitute the fundamental dialectic of Jewish messianism. The restorative impulse looks backward to a lost wholeness; the utopian impulse reaches toward a future that breaks with all precedents. Both, however, share the conviction that the present order is not final and that a decisive transformation is possible.[^3]

For **Moshe Idel**, this binary is insufficient. Idel distinguishes between two fundamentally different models of messianism. The **[[2. Main Theoretical Pillars/Theory Notes/Luria's theosophical-theurgical Tikkun\|theosophical-theurgical]]** model, dominant in [[3. Concepts/Luria's Safed Circle\|Lurianic Kabbalah]], locates redemption in the repair of the divine realm itself: human beings perform commandments with mystical intention (*[[3. Concepts/kavanah\|kavanah]]*) in order to restore the shattered godhead, and the Messiah is the figure who enters creation as a result of completes this cosmic work.[^4] The **ecstatic** model, by contrast, locates redemption in the transformation of human consciousness: the messianic is an experience available in the present, accessed through techniques of unification, ascent, and the stripping away of corporeality.[^5] Idel identifies a third, **talismanic** model as well, in which the righteous individual draws down divine power through ritual acts that affect the world without theurgical transformation of the godhead.[^6]

The key phenomenological shift Idel traces is the move, within Hasidism, from a messianism of deferred hope to a messianism of present experience: "The Hasidic emphasis on experience should be understood as a move from hope to experience. In the ecstatic and Hasidic treatments of messianism we may presuppose the availability of the messianic mode of existence which offers an immediate experience as much informed by hope as by real attempts to actualize the messianic potentiality of religious life."[^7] Here, the messianic is not a future event to be awaited but a mode of being that can be inhabited now. The *Baal Shem Tov* reports the Messiah's answer to his question "When do you come?": "When your doctrine will be revealed in public... and also they will be able to perform the unifications and the ascents as you do, and then the shells will be abolished and there will be a time of good will and redemption."[^8] Redemption is not a dramatic rupture in history but a cumulative, experiential process—the dissemination of a practice, the perfection of the soul, the unification of speech and thought.

## Secularised Messianism: Benjamin, Adorno, Derrida

The messianic idea does not remain confined to religious thought. In the twentieth century, a number of Jewish thinkers extracted the structure of messianic expectation from its theological context and deployed it within philosophy, politics, and cultural critique.

For **Walter Benjamin**, the messianic is not a future moment on the timeline of history but a "weak messianic power" that inhabits every present: each generation is endowed with a claim upon the past, and the task of historical materialism is "to blast open the continuum of history" by seizing hold of a memory that flashes up at a moment of danger.[^9] The messianic is the arrest of homogeneous empty time, the "now" (*Jetztzeit*) in which the past is redeemed and the present becomes a "time of the now."[^10] Benjamin's messianism is neither restorative nor utopian in Scholem's sense; it is a revolutionary interruption that breaks the chain of catastrophe.

For **Theodor W. Adorno**, the messianic enters as a negative regulative idea. "The whole is the false," and no positive image of redemption can be offered without betraying it; yet the very capacity to recognise the false as false depends on a messianic perspective—"the standpoint of redemption"—which philosophy must adopt without claiming to possess it.[^11] Adorno and Horkheimer, in *Dialectic of Enlightenment*, diagnose the late-modern condition as one in which the promise of Exodus—the Enlightenment as *Ausgang* out of self-incurred immaturity—has been betrayed by its reversion into myth. Yet they refuse to relinquish hope: "hope is given to us only for the sake of the hopeless," they write, echoing Benjamin.[^12] Despair is not the end of hope but the condition of its possibility—a dialectical insight they set against both the facile optimism of progress and the apocalyptic temptation to renounce the world entirely.

For **Jacques Derrida**, the messianic is stripped of all determinate content. He distinguishes between **messianism** (the historical, religious traditions that await a specific redeemer) and **messianicity** (a universal structure of experience, the opening to a future that is radically unpredictable, unprogrammable, "to-come"). Messianicity is "the coming of the other, the absolute and unpredictable singularity of the arrivant as justice."[^13] It has no Messiah, no positive content, no horizon of expectation. It is the bare structure of openness to what cannot be anticipated.

## Agamben: Messianic Time and the Inoperativity of Law

**Giorgio Agamben** develops his concept of messianism primarily through a reading of Paul's Letter to the Romans. For Agamben, messianic time is not the end of time but "the time of the end"—a time that inhabits chronological time while suspending it from within.[^14] It is neither the future nor the past, neither chronological time (*chronos*) nor eternity (*eschaton*), but "a zone of absolute indiscernibility between immanence and transcendence, between this world and the future world."[^15]

The messianic, for Agamben, is characterised by the **inoperativity** of the law. It does not annul the law; it renders it inoperative—suspending its force without destroying its form. Paul's *hos me* ("as if not") is the paradigmatic messianic gesture: to live in the world as though one were not of it, to use the law without being used by it.[^16] The messianic subject is the "remnant"—that which remains when the law's divisions have been divided again, suspending the suspension.[^17]

Agamben also applies the Schmittian category of the **state of exception** to messianism. The sovereign, for Schmitt, is the one who decides on the exception—who suspends the normative order in the name of its preservation. Agamben identifies this as a secularised messianic structure: the sovereign occupies the position of the messianic figure, but in doing so produces **bare life**—life exposed to power without legal protection, "life that may be killed and yet not sacrificed."[^18] The *homo sacer* is the product of this exteriorised messianic logic: he is the one upon whom the sovereign ban falls, included solely through his exclusion from the protection of the law.

## Apocalyptic Ideation and the Culture of the End

**Agata Bielik-Robson** situates the messianic within the broader Western tradition of apocalyptic thought. Drawing on Jacob Taubes's distinction between the apocalyptic and the katechonic, she describes two fundamental postures toward the end: one that longs for the dissolution of the present order (*fiat iustitia, pereat mundus*—"let justice be done, though the world perish") and one that seeks to preserve the world against the forces of chaos (*fiat mundus, pereat iustitia*—"let the world stand, though justice perish").[^19] The apocalyptic mood, she argues, has become hegemonic in late modernity: "we live in a 'culture of the end'" in which "the concept of the end [...] lost its ambivalent hue" and the "dysteleological tendency of *Naturdämmerung* overshadowed the positive image of the *telos* as the Omega-point of fulfilment."[^20]

Christian eschatology provides the deep structure of this Western apocalyptic ideation. The *eschaton*—the final event in the divine plan, the Last Judgment, the end of history—functions as the horizon against which all temporal experience is measured. In its Christian articulation, the end is simultaneously catastrophe and consummation: the destruction of the sinful world and the inauguration of the New Jerusalem.[^21] This ambiguity, Bielik-Robson argues, has been disambiguated in the modern period. The discovery of entropy, the scientific "death of the universe" prophesied by Carnot, Clausius, and Helmholtz, tipped the balance toward pure catastrophe: the end became not *telos* but termination, not fulfilment but exhaustion. The "new religion of science," as Agamben calls it, "inherits not only the form of the prophetic discourse, but also its violent negation of the finite fragile being as essentially unworthy of existence."[^22]

Against this apocalyptic disambiguation, Bielik-Robson recovers a counter-tradition: Benjamin's "nihilism as world politics," the "quietest approach" that would disarm the apocalyptic mood by embracing transience without vengeance—accepting the entropic passing of the world without calling down destruction upon it.[^23] This is not messianic hope in the strong sense but a "hope in reverse," a willingness to tarry with finitude rather than hasten its end.

## Summary

Messianism, in its historical range, is the expectation of a decisive transformation—brought by a redeemer, an event, or a shift in consciousness—that will fulfil or suspend history as it is known. In its Lurianic form, it is cosmic and theurgical; in its Hasidic reformulation, it becomes experiential and immanent. In its secularised philosophical forms (Benjamin, Adorno, Derrida, Agamben), it becomes a structure of temporality, a relation to the law, or an ethical openness to the future. In the apocalyptic tradition analysed by Bielik-Robson, it generates the deep cultural logic by which Western modernity oscillates between hope for a *telos* and despair at an ending that promises nothing beyond itself.
