According to R. Nahman, "simple faith" is a non-dialectical mode of religious devotion intended for everyone except the most elevated spiritual leader (the _zaddik_ of the generation). It is characterized by a complete rejection of philosophical speculation and reason as a basis for worship, requiring instead that God be served with pure simplicity. This faith avoids any confrontation with the rationally irresolvable questions of heresy that arise from the divine void, a struggle reserved exclusively for the _zaddik_ who possesses a "dialectical faith."

>R. Nahman maintained that the first type of faith should be that of everyone except the zaddik of the generation, a group that included R. Nahman, one presumes. It is a faith that rejects philosophical speculation and negates the use of reason as the matrix of religious worship. R. Nahman strongly argued that the one who adheres to simple faith must discard reason: "Truly, reason ought to be cast aside and all cleverness discarded—God must be worshipped in simplicity." ==The second type of faith requires interaction with the heretic, a knowledge of philosophy, and thus the constant struggle to overcome the heretical question. This second, dialectical type of faith brings forth suffering and anguish; it is the faith of the zaddik, for only he can truly overcome the absence of God and reach the experience of the transcendent God.==

Shaul Magid, “Through the Void: The Absence of God in R. Nahman of Bratzlav's Likkutei MoHaRan,” _Harvard Theological Review_ 88, no. 4 (1995): 504 (fn. 26-29 omitted)

>R. Nahman's view of faith is complex, and a full treatment of his highly nuanced approach is beyond the scope of this present study. It is important, however, to note that he spoke of two distinct types of faith, the first a "simple" faith, the second a "dialectical" faith. R. Nahman maintained that the first type of faith should be that of everyone except the zaddik of the generation, a group that included R. Nahman, one presumes. It is a faith that rejects philosophical speculation and negates the use of reason as the matrix of religious worship. R. Nahman strongly argued that the one who adheres to simple faith must discard reason: "Truly, reason ought to be cast aside and all cleverness discarded—God must be worshipped in simplicity." The second type of faith requires interaction with the heretic, a knowledge of philosophy, and thus the constant struggle to overcome the heretical question. This second, dialectical type of faith brings forth suffering and anguish; it is the faith of the zaddik, for only he can truly overcome the absence of God and reach the experience of the transcendent God.

Shaul Magid, “Through the Void: The Absence of God in R. Nahman of Bratzlav's Likkutei MoHaRan,” _Harvard Theological Review_ 88, no. 4 (1995): 504.

>It appears, however, that the faith presented in R. Nahman's lessons is a dialectical one and not a simple faith that would retreat from any confrontation with the void. For the adherent of simple faith, the whole exegetical enterprise is largely useless, as is the possibility of experiencing the transcendent God.

Shaul Magid, “Through the Void: The Absence of God in R. Nahman of Bratzlav's Likkutei MoHaRan,” _Harvard Theological Review_ 88, no. 4 (1995): 505.

>With regard to R. Nahman's "simple faith," it appears that reason plays only a negative role and thus would fall outside of Pascal's claim. In R. Nahman's dialectical faith, however, reason—depicted as the experience of the absence of God—is represented by the second heretic, the one who is rooted in the void and poses the unanswerable question to the seeker of divine presence.

Shaul Magid, “Through the Void: The Absence of God in R. Nahman of Bratzlav's Likkutei MoHaRan,” _Harvard Theological Review_ 88, no. 4 (1995): 517.

R. Nahman of Bratzlav, _Likkutei MoHaRaN_ I, Torah 64, end of letter 2, as cited in Joseph Weiss, “The Empty Space, Sabbateanism, and its Melody,” in _Kabbalah: Journal for the Study of Jewish Mystical Texts_ 15 (2006): 209.

R. Nahman of Bratzlav, _Likkutei MoHaRaN_ I, Torah 64, letter 3, as cited in Joseph Weiss, “The Empty Space, Sabbateanism, and its Melody,” in _Kabbalah: Journal for the Study of Jewish Mystical Texts_ 15 (2006): 209–210.
