The development of Kabbalah extended from previous, non-kabbalistic esoteric literature (in the third to sixth centuries the Sefer Yetzirah, in the first to tenth centuries the Merkavah / Hekhalot literature) to the first proto-kabbalistic work, the Book Bahir, written ca. 1185 in Provence. Through the 12th to 13th centuries the gerona circles, which included Nachmanides, developed towards early Zoharic Kabbalah. The Zohar,  penned by Moses de Leon and being the first major kabbalistic work, is considered by many in Orthodoxy to stand beside the Talmud and the Torah as a foundational work of Judaism. It was conceived under interesting (albeit typical) conditions, having been presented as neither written by its author nor in his time, and even composed in an artificial Aramaic invented by de Leon in order to hide his authorship. These disguises allowed de Leon to write without the restrictions of his times.[^1]

Among other things, the Zohar emphasises the concept of reflection, all things being reflections of all other things. The universe therefore bears the structural imprint of the divine realms. Consequently, all events within the universe—spanning past and present—find their parallel in the mythological dynamics of the divine powers. In this way, everything becomes a metaphor for everything else. [[3. Concepts/Messianism\|Interestingly for our further consideration, it is in this sense that the messianic era, the ultimate redemption, is included within the operations of the inner self.]][^2]
