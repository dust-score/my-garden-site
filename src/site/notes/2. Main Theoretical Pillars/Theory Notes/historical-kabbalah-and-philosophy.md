The development of Kabbalah is considered by some to have been an ecstatic, experiential response to the Jewish philosophical movement of the 10th-12th centuries, prominently the rationalist, Aristotelian synthesis of Judaism put forward by Moses Maimonides’ in his _Guide for the Perplexed_.

Notably, however, its entry point into the elucidation of the innermost divine realms bears a marked resemblance to that of the rationalistic philosophers it delineated itself in opposition to.[^1] This connection is visible in the concept _[[3. Concepts/Eyn Sof\|Eyn Sof]]_, a stand-in term denoting infinity, the absence of beginning or end, or perhaps a supreme entity, echoing Aristotelian thought on the “primal cause” or “unmoved mover”.

[^1]:  Dan, 2006, p. 39
