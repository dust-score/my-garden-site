According to Scholem it was in relation to these antinomian [[2. Main Theoretical Pillars/Theory Notes/rupture\|ruptures]] that Hasidism came to neutralise the [[3. Concepts/Messianism\|messianic]] tension inherent to [[2. Main Theoretical Pillars/Theory Notes/Luria's theosophical-theurgical Tikkun\|Lurianic tikkun]] by exchanging it for the concept of devequt, adherence to or union with God. This is at least a highly simplified account of Scholem’s initial analysis - later contended by Taubes and then Idel. It was Taubes’ contestation that rather than doing away with messianism entirely, Hasidism has broadly “turned it inwards” ([[3. Concepts/implication\|implicated it]]).[^1] Idel, further, disagrees with any generalisations of messianism in Hasidism and speaks of multiplicities of messianisms, foregrounding ecstatic and talismanic forms of messianism over the theosophical theurgical model, which appears to have been sidelined in favour of the others. Relevant to this thesis is the development of  Hasidic messianism away from a hope for future resolution and towards an emphasis on experience, seen in the ecstatic and talismanic forms. 

“[W]here full-fledged messianism [...] emerges as a transition from present experience to hope"[^2], in the Hasidic model, the agentic quality of the messianic can be implemented within the present experience of the individual rather than exclusively relegrated to a future which must thereby be conjured up.

_"In the ecstatic and Hasidic treatments of messianism we may presuppose the availability of the messianic mode of existence which offers an immediate experience as much informed by hope as by real attempts to actualize the messianic potentiality of religious life."_ It is in this way that a “messianic mode of being” can be assumed.
