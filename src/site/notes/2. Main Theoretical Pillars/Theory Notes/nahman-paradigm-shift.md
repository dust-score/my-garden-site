Nahman’s sensitivity lies precisely in an understanding of the existential suffering implied in the pursuit of God despite the experience of his absence. As such, he represents a broader paradigm shift, of which he was unaware, in which the medieval sense of God’s _incomprehensibility_ gave way to the more modern experience of  humanity’s _estrangement_ from God. This shift, in turn, reflects the contemporaneous  reorientation of western modern thought from the cosmos to the individual.[^1]

[^1]: “from the medieval period’s sense of the incomprehensibility of God to the human estrangement from God [which] may be seen in light of the shift in orientation of early modern thought from the cosmos to the individual.”  
	SEE Magid P. 496
