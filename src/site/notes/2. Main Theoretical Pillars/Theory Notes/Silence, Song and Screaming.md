>Thought is a level that exceeds speech; not an image that merely expresses a negative vacuum, it is rather a metaphor for a phase that precedes and transcends speech, and for this reason is beyond human comprehension. Humans, including Moses, cannot ‘answer’ a place in which there is no speech with speech – only with silence. 
>(Zvi Mark, Silence and Melody Facing the Void, 160)

[[5. Citations/6. Papers/Weiss, The Empty Space; Sabbateanism and its Melodies\|Weiss]] quotes the Tale of the Seven Beggars, showing how Nahman proposed to respond to the non verbal silence.

> "he, through his silence, resolves everything through the fact that he is merely silent (for he does only some silence and it is a resolution for everything). Hence, his silence is the little that holds much." (Weiss, p. 13)

It is Nahman's belief that when the _zaddik_ is of the aspect of Moses, that is to say possessing an aspect of the inarticulable, which is interior access to the messianic, he must dedicate himself to that heretical thought which engages with the absolute paradox.

> "But know, if there is a great _zadik_, [[2. Main Theoretical Pillars/The Messianic Trap#^moses\|who is of the aspect of Moses]], he specifically needs to study these heretical matters. And even though it is impossible to resolve them as mentioned, nonetheless, through his study that he studies there, he raises up from there several souls that fell and sank into this heresy." (Weiss, p. 8)

Weiss goes on to suggest that the heresies with which Nahman engaged included those of the antinomian Sabbatean sect.

> "I propose, as a working hypothesis, that these heretical writings to which R. Nahman hints are none other than the writings of extreme Sabbatean theology, teaching the doctrine of the incarnation of God in the [[2. Main Theoretical Pillars/The Messianic Trap#^heresy\|messianic king]]. This heretical doctrine fulfills the aforementioned definition, i.e., non-existence of God within the world and His existence within it despite everything." (Weiss, p. 29)

This is relevant to me because it shows a dichotomy in the dealing with the absolute paradox: on the one hand, Nahman says that when the _tsaddik_ of his generation is of the aspect of Moses he holds some messianic capacity within himself. On the other hand, the Sabbatean heretical answer, from which the fallen sparks are to be liberated, sought ultimate messianic incarnation in a redeemer figure (in this case, Sabbatai Zvi). 

Weiss too, in his analysis, supposes an act of deferment in Nahman's engagement with the void. The melody is the expression of the reconciliation of irreconcilable paradox which occurs only in a future [[2. Main Theoretical Pillars/Theory Notes/wolfson-time-becoming\|messianic time]]; now is the time of a silence which faces the unutterable on its terms.

> "The great silent one will be the great poet of the future to come... The eschatological dialectic of this zadik is that in him is fulfilled the transformation of the silence of now into the messianic song of the future to come." (Weiss, p. 17)

Zvi Mark, in _Silence and Melody facing the Void_, contends otherwise. 

> "Despite the central role the subject of the melody plays in the confrontation with the void, it is absent from the studies of [[5. Citations/6. Papers/Weiss, The Empty Space; Sabbateanism and its Melodies\|Weiss]], Green and [[5. Citations/6. Papers/Magid - 1995 - Through the Void The Absence of God in R. Naḥman of Bratzlav's Likkutei MoHaRan\|Magid]], who discuss Teaching 64 and the void extensively. Weiss, who extensively examined the meaning of silence and its different strata, completely ignores the topic of the melody, even though he writes at length in different places about Teaching 64 and the void." ([[2. Main Theoretical Pillars/Theory Notes/citations from ch 4 mystical madness\|Zvi Mark, Silence and Melody Facing the Void]], p. 167)

Magid, in _Through the Void_ (1995) explicates another techne in Nahman's phenomenological approach to the Empty Space. This is the [[2. Main Theoretical Pillars/Theory Notes/Implication of the Scream\|scream]].

>“The aspect of Moses is not restricted to silence; it also comprises melody, the mystical ability to come in contact with ‘the light of ‘Eyn Sof,’ even when the latter is not manifested in intellect, language, and speech. The belief of the zaddik who is of the aspect of Moses is depicted as a mystical ability to know or come in contact with ‘the light of ‘Eyn Sof,’ in a way different from that of wisdom and intellect.” (Ibid, p. 164)

>This conception is portrayed in a circular structure: every created being and wisdom has a melody and wisdom that are higher than it, that encompass and vitalize it, and by force of which ‘this wisdom is derived.’ Thus, it is possible to ascend from one wisdom to another, from one level to the next, until one reaches the beginning of Creation. (Ibid, p. 163)

This is because all melodies, including the heretical ones, are aspects of the highest melody, which is of divinity.

>By expressing the melodies of joy, a person can attain belief, _devekut_ (adherence to God), the spirit of divine inspiration, and prophecy. This understanding well suits the description of melody as a way to attain ‘the light of ‘Eyn Sof,’ while the intellect, wisdom, and speech cannot serve as a vehicle for the attainment of the ‘light of ‘Eyn Sof itself.’ (Ibid, 164)

Distinguishing the [[2. Main Theoretical Pillars/Theory Notes/Implication of the Scream\|scream]] from the melody, we land upon structure and differentiation. The scream appears akin to the paradox in that it is non-verbal and inflective: it articulates a non-articulable question which by its expressive force appears as an answer which [[1. Surface Texts/One Story of Finitude#^scream\|constitutes itself as a question]]. The melody is perhaps some type of _[[3. Concepts/Turning Around\|resolution]]_ of the nonverbal. It is that which expresses meaning without necessary recourse to the spoken word, superceding the paradox in more cohering fashion than the scream. I speculate for a moment that perhaps the scream also [[3. Concepts/implication\|implicates]] a type of [[2. Main Theoretical Pillars/Theory Notes/rupture\|rupture]].

As it is the one who is of the aspect of Moses who engages the paradox by way of its embodiment, uttering without articulation, what occurs here is an act of Tikkun. This is the [[2. Main Theoretical Pillars/Theory Notes/wolfson-time-becoming\|inflection]] of the inarticulable, a [[2. Main Theoretical Pillars/The Messianic Trap#^profanation\|profanation]] in Agamben's sense, through which that which cannot be overcome is [[3. Concepts/Methodology of Implication\|enacted]] and thereby exceeded infinitesimally. 

It is here that we can now turn to Nahman’s scream: the action, practice of implication of the Other, the Real, the unnameable, the question, the contradiction into the self as an act of profanation of language through which emerges the reconstitution of the question: the nonverbal potentiality of motion into a hitherto impossible position or state.

>The scream brings one past the darkness, through the void to the presence of God as creator by giving birth to a new state of consciousness, moving from the wordless to the word.[[9. Footnotes/Citations from Citations/A\|A]] This scream is intended to force the individual to confront divine absence and to produce a way beyond that absence. (p. 507,508)
