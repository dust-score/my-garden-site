Kabbalah is the 13th century continuation of older Jewish esoteric traditions. These enacted approaches through language to that which it was prohibited to speak upon, which was prominently[^1]  the question of the beginning and ending of things.[^2] This is a question of finitude, on the one hand attempting to find an answer to the question of mutable existence in the face of the convincing alternative, non-existence, or unchanging and eternal divinity. On the other hand, Kabbalah has also, in its variations, been interpreted as reflective of one of the painful expressions of finitude, the suffering of history. [^6]

Up until the middle ages, Kabbalah was a term in the Hebrew religious vocabulary denoting “nonindividual, nonexperiential religious truth, which is received by tradition”.[^7]  In the late twelfth century, an additional meaning was added, as early Kabbalists began to contribute esoteric strata of meaning to the pre-existing Jewish tradition. The development of Kabbalah intensified, systematised and renamed previous motions, claiming the revelation of that which had been hidden to earlier generations — through renewed obfuscation, legible only to select initiates. Within this period there was almost no recognition of individual experiences of direct contact with the divine[^8].  This, too, was a determining factor for the development of a culture of hidden interpretation, in which meaning was guarded behind layers of obfuscation. A notable exception in this was Abraham Abulafia, who pursued a more solitary mysticism and differed on several counts from his contemporaries.

The development of Kabbalah is considered by some to have been an ecstatic, experiential response to the Jewish philosophical movement of the 10th-12th centuries, prominently the rationalist, Aristotelian synthesis of Judaism put forward by Moses Maimonides’ in his _Guide for the Perplexed_.

Notably, however, its entry point into the elucidation of the innermost divine realms bears a marked resemblance to that of the rationalistic philosophers it delineated itself in opposition to.[^9] This connection is visible in the concept _Eyn Sof_, a stand-in term denoting infinity, the absence of beginning or end, or perhaps a supreme entity, echoing Aristotelian thought on the “primal cause” or “unmoved mover”.

The development of Kabbalah extended from previous, non-kabbalistic esoteric literature (in the third to sixth centuries the Sefer Yetzirah, in the first to tenth centuries the Merkavah / Hekhalot literature) to the first proto-kabbalistic work, the Book Bahir, written ca. 1185 in Provence. Through the 12th to 13th centuries the Gerona circles, which included Nachmanides, developed towards early Zoharic Kabbalah. The Zohar, written by Moses de Leon and being the first major kabbalistic work, is considered by many in Orthodoxy to stand beside the Talmud and the Torah as a foundational work of Judaism. It was conceived under interesting (albeit typical) conditions, having been presented as neither written by its author nor in his time, and even composed in an artificial Aramaic invented by de Leon in order to hide his authorship. These disguises allowed de Leon to write without the restrictions of his times.[^10]

Among other things, the Zohar emphasises the concept of reflection, all things being reflections of all other  things. The universe therefore bears the structural imprint of the divine realms. Consequently, all events within the universe—spanning past and present—find their parallel in the mythological dynamics of the divine powers. In this way, everything becomes a metaphor for everything else. Interestingly for our further consideration, it is in this sense that the messianic era, the ultimate redemption, is included within the operations of the inner self.[^11]

It is a Scholemian claim[^12] that the persecution of Spanish Jewry in the fifteenth century, culminating in the exile of the Jews from Spain in 1492, transformed the spiritual climate within the Jewish communities, leading to the decline of the previously dominant rationalist philosophy and a growing interest in Kabbalah. It was in this context of intensely messianic and apocalyptic inclinations that several kabbalistic circles developed.

This initiated a process that, by the sixteenth century, would elevate Kabbalah from the purview of isolated esoteric groups to the foremost spiritual doctrine of the Jewish people in early modern times.
