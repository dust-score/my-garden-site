Kabbalah is the 13th century continuation of older Jewish esoteric traditions. These enacted approaches through language to that which it was prohibited to speak upon, which was prominently[^1] the question of the beginning and ending of things.[^2]

This is a question of [[3. Concepts/On Finitude\|finitude]], on the one hand attempting to find an answer to the question of mutable existence in the face of the convincing alternative, non-existence, or unchanging and eternal divinity. On the other hand, Kabbalah has also, in its variations, been interpreted as reflective of one of the painful expressions of finitude, the suffering of history.[^3] 

Up until the middle ages, Kabbalah was a term in the Hebrew religious vocabulary denoting “nonindividual, nonexperiential religious truth, which is received by tradition”.[^4]  In the late twelfth century, an additional meaning was added, as early Kabbalists began to contribute esoteric strata of meaning to the pre-existing Jewish tradition. The development of Kabbalah intensified, systematised and renamed previous motions, claiming the revelation of that which had been hidden to earlier generations — through renewed obfuscation, legible only to select initiates. Within this period there was almost no recognition of individual experiences of direct contact with the divine.[^5] This, too, was a determining factor for the development of a culture of hidden interpretation, in which meaning was guarded behind layers of obfuscation. A notable exception in this was Abraham Abulafia, who pursued a more solitary mysticism and differed on several counts from his contemporaries.
