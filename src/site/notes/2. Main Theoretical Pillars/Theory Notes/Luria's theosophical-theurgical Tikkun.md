[[2. Main Theoretical Pillars/Theory Notes/persecution-messianism\|persecution-messianism]]

In Luria’s theosophical-theurgical model, tikkun seemed fairly conservative: prayer, observing the commandments, and thereby, through the systematic interrelation with the divine, contributing collectively to its restitution. Perhaps due to the pressures this exerted on individuals in the narrative of cosmic redemption however, the implications of tikkun in Lurianic Kabbalah soon became functional in the acceleration of messianic narratives, in which the agentic function of tikkun was outsourced to a messiah, who would embody the necessity of tikkun - thereby supplanting the need for individual redemption.[^1] As the persecution of the European Jews continued to compound throughout the sixteenth century, messianism became paramount not only in esoteric circles but in broader Jewish society, finally being used in the legitimation of two major antinomian ruptures within Jewish esotericism.

[^1]:  messianic mystics, idel, pg. 36
