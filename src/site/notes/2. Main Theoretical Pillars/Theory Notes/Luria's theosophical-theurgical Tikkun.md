[[3. Concepts/Messianism\|Messianism]]
[[2. Main Theoretical Pillars/Theory Notes/persecution-messianism\|persecution-messianism]]

In Luria’s theosophical-theurgical model, tikkun seemed fairly conservative: prayer, observing the commandments, and thereby, through the systematic interrelation with the divine, contributing collectively to its restitution. Perhaps due to the pressures this exerted on individuals in the narrative of cosmic redemption however, the implications of tikkun in Lurianic Kabbalah soon became functional in the acceleration of messianic narratives, in which the agentic function of tikkun was outsourced to a messiah, who would embody the necessity of tikkun - thereby supplanting the need for individual redemption.[^1] According to Scholem it was in relation to these antinomian ruptures that Hasidism came to neutralise the messianic tension inherent to Lurianic tikkun by exchanging it for the concept of _devequt_, adherence to or union with God. This is at least a highly simplified account of Scholem’s initial analysis—later contended by Taubes and then Idel. It was Taubes’ contestation that rather than doing away with messianism entirely, Hasidism has broadly “turned it inwards” (implicated it). 

Idel, further, disagrees with any generalisations of messianism in Hasidism and speaks of multiplicities of messianisms, foregrounding ecstatic and talismanic forms of messianism over the theosophical-theurgical model, which appears to have been sidelined in favour of the others. Relevant to this thesis is the development of Hasidic messianism away from a hope for future resolution and towards an emphasis on experience, seen in the ecstatic and talismanic forms.

[^1]:  messianic mystics, idel, pg. 36
