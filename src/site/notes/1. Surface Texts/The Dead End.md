Whether the [[1. Surface Texts/dead end\|dead end]] can be satisfactorily conceptualised as the impossibility of the  reconciliation of opposing narratives or of bearing the  unbearable, there is a dead end. It is not singular in its presentation: though they may grow from a distant body, all I see are the many heads of the Hydra. This body then is the unutterable and inexplicable, as each head tells me a different tale. It requires an engagement with the unanswerable, that which causes untenable tension when engaged. This is a tension that can immobilise movement, suspend between interpretations, overwhelm with complexity, and confound narrative.

There is an apocalyptic ideation endemic to Western thought. It is a cultural  framework for finitude with a tendency of deferring the agentic quality of the present to a u- or dys- topian future — the “end of history.” A What remains within the unloved present is the question of dealing with that which would so much rather be relegated to a resolved future or a past, overcome: contradiction.[^1]

In “Tikkun Viewed from the Perspective of Systems Philosophy,” Martin Zwick expresses finitude, in Lurianic Kabbalah the breaking of the vessels, as a necessary prerequisite for the existence of such a complex system as life on earth.

Without the flaw experienced as the dead end, there could be no motion, therefore there could only be stasis: the eternal eschaton. It is therefore an interesting tension that life requires entropy, requires its own limitation, and that this existence within a limited state gives rise to the necessity of Tikkun, repair, or rectification—and yet that Tikkun itself is conceptualised as actualising the eschaton, the cessation of finitude and therefore of motion within time, ergo (mortal, physical) life itself.

Imagining the actualisation of the eschaton, too, implies the concatenation of oppositional conceptions of tikkun, as it occurs by means of intensification, which may run along interiorised or exteriorised matrices, with varying degrees of interrelation between the two. Hereby we arrive at divergent implications in regards to the nature of tikkun: when exteriorised as the actualisation of earthly conditions prerequisite for the arrival of the Messiah, tikkun can mean something wholly different than when understood as the realisation of the reality of the Eschaton within the self. My consideration of tikkun can only represent a measly square inch in the vast field of thought regarding the topic.
