In the face of the unanswerable question, heretical thought is a potential consequence of the engagement with the paradox of God’s absence/presence despite the experience of his presence/absence. Heresy, understood for our secular purposes more broadly as the wilful abnegation of moral codes, may be understood as the inverse of _simple faith_: as we are perspectivally incapable of knowing truth we may as well spend our lives in the furtherance of our own egoic interests. It is Nahman’s belief that when the _zaddik_ (community leader) is of the aspect of Moses, that is to say possessing an aspect of the inarticulable, and therefore an interior access to the messianic, he must dedicate himself to that heretical thought which engages with the absolute paradox.

But know, if there is a great zadik, who is of the aspect of Moses, he specifically needs to study these heretical matters. And even though it is impossible to resolve them as mentioned, nonetheless, through his study that he studies there, he raises up from there several souls that fell and sank into this heresy.

  
If the experience of the absence of God tempts heresy, and the blind maintenance of belief in the immanence of God despite indications to the contrary is simple faith, Nahman’s proposition is that of a non-resolution, a so-called _dialectical faith_. If it is accepted that paradox is an aspect of the experience of existence and will never be resolved, the paradox can itself become an aspect of faith, thereby reconstituting the experience subjected to suffering under the conditions of the paradox. For the ordinary person, this implies a relationship with doubt, with the existential pain of narrative insecurity. The dialectical faith of the ordinary person is held and navigated with the help of the _zaddik_, who embraces the suffering individual in the times of their difficulties, allowing them to recognise the hidden divine within their absmal states, their smallness. The _zaddik_ is one who can find God in the _klippot_ (husks), without becoming trapped there.

  

I propose, as a working hypothesis, that these heretical writings to which R. Nahman hints are none other than the writings of extreme Sabbatean theology, teaching _the doctrine of the incarnation of God in the messianic king_. This heretical doctrine fulfills the aforementioned definition, i.e., non-existence of God within the world and His existence within it despite everything. _(emphasis mine)_  

The Sabbatean answer to the “absolute difficulty” sought redemption in a redeemer figure, the incarnation of God in the “messianic king”. The conversion of their supposed messiah to Islam (1666), led Sabbatai Zvi’s followers, directed by their prophet, Nathan of Gaza, to declare the arrival of messianic time, and consequently sanction an inversion of the Law. 

  

Born roughtly one century after Zvi’s conversion, Nahman recognised the danger of the Sabbatean doctrine, and its potential for seduction, and in part as a response to it adapted the role of the _zaddik_ to imply a particular relationship with the heretical: an agentic capacity for descent, discerned in nature from the chaotic fall into darkness that the individual “not of the aspect of Moses” risks when engaging with heresy. 

  

The _zaddik_ directs the messianic capacity without claims to messianic incarnation; they possess the capacity to hold their people in their darkness — affording them the strength to resist seduction by external redeemer narratives.

  

Within Nahmans comogony of paradox, the silent void, i.e. the empty space, is “not a lacuna” between the possibility of the immanent and transcendent God, 

but the possibility of the _nonexistence_ of the transcendent God, which makes the immanent God an illusion. This is not to say that R. Nahman came to such a conclusion; his life's work attempted to avert such heresy and disbelief. He began, however, with the assumption that the heretical question must be taken seriously if it is to be overcome. Moreover, only by confronting this question can one achieve an experience of the transcendent God.

_(emphasis mine)_

  

By way of a sort of phenomenological imitation of the void, techne are found through which the void may be bridged and the transcendent God may be experienced.

  

Thought is a level that exceeds speech; not an image that merely expresses a negative vacuum, it is rather a metaphor for a phase that precedes and transcends speech, and for this reason is beyond human comprehension. Humans, including Moses, cannot ‘answer’ a place in which there is no speech with speech—only with silence.  

The void and silence are structurally related in that they both represent an absence of articulation, (with-)held in its pre-articulate form in a state of potential. Herefore, silence can be seen as a performance of the unutterable, unanswerable, i.e. the void.

In relating Nahman’s engagement with the silent void, Weiss quotes from _The Tale of the Seven Beggars:_

he, through his silence, resolves everything through the fact that he is merely silent (for he does only some silence and it is a resolution for everything). Hence, his silence is the little that holds much.

  

The silence exists as a contrast to melody, which originates from the point of contact between the finite world and the infinite divine. Weiss, in his analysis, supposes an act of deferment in Nahman’s engagement with the void. Melody will be the expression of the reconciliation of irreconcilable paradox which occurs only in a future messianic time; now is the time of a silence which faces the unutterable on its terms.

The great silent one will be the great poet of the future to come… The eschatological dialectic of this zadik is that in him is fulfilled the transformation of the silence of now into the messianic song of the future to come.  

Magid, in _Through the Void_ (1995), adds a further techne to Nahman’s arsenal. In this, the scream becomes the wordless, pre-articulate cry that breaks through the silence of the void and enables the birth of prayer. It is the point where the _t_s_imtsum_ of the heart—the contraction of unbridled desire—crosses into articulated speech.

The scream serves as a midwife, as it were, which, reflecting the exilic character of the shekhina, yields a new level of consciousness…  

In this way the “leaping over” of the paradox becomes as much a part of reality as is the experience of the paradox, i.e. of finitude.

The process occurs thus: an existential response to the experience of God’s absence wells up. This is yearning. One must be aware of the lack. This desire is parted so as to create an absence within it. This is _tsimtsum_. Finally, the scream emanates through the heart’s void in a thin line, allowing the introduction of the unutterble in to the realm of silence. Articulation emerges from the distinction which has now occurred.

Rectification fails if the process is not completed:

First, one must not allow the emotions embedded in one’s heart to go unattended, thus avoiding confrontations of divine absence. This is the realm of eyn sof where creation, prayer and divine service (_avodah_) are impossible. Second, one must avoid only confronting the emptiness and not completing the creative process by actually elevating the yearning for God in prayer. The first danger will lead to antinomian heresy… The second danger will yield to fatalism: the individual will become overcome by the absence, seeing no possibility of redemption, and thus will be trapped in the snares of the second heretic.  

The scream is thus a mode of Tikkun: the implication of the non-articulable within an articulation, or the “bringing together” of that which is disparate.

Despite the central role the subject of the melody plays in the confrontation with the void, it is absent from the studies of Weiss, Green and Magid, who discuss Teaching 64 and the void extensively. Weiss, who extensively examined the meaning of silence and its different strata, completely ignores the topic of the melody, even though he writes at length in different places about Teaching 64 and the void.

  

Here we can put down a brief simplification in order to orient ourselves. Silence corresponds to the void. The scream emanates from the point at which the infinite touches the finite. Finally, melody is of the realm of Eyn Sof, the ineffable beyond the realm of finitude. Melody, too, enacts resolution - albeit on a different register than the scream.

The aspect of Moses is not restricted to silence; it also comprises melody, the mystical ability to come in contact with ‘the light of ’Eyn Sof,’ even when the latter is not manifested in intellect, language, and speech. The belief of the _zaddik_ who is of the aspect of Moses is depicted as a mystical ability to know or come in contact with ‘the light of ’Eyn Sof,’ in  a way different from that of wisdom and intellect.  

This is because all melodies, including the heretical ones, are aspects of the highest melody, which is of divinity. o reach the origin, the highest melody of Creation, means only to ascend from one melody to the next. Melody is joy; the scream enacts an overcoming but the melody enacts the wordless coherence that emerges from the inarticulate.

By expressing the melodies of joy, a person can attain belief, _devekut_ (adherence to God), the spirit of divine inspiration, and prophecy. This understanding well suits the description of melody as a way to attain ‘the light of ’Eyn Sof,’ while the intellect, wisdom, and speech cannot serve as a vehicle for the attainment of the ‘light of ’Eyn Sof itself.’  

Distinguishing the scream from the melody, we land upon structure and differentiation. The scream appears akin to the paradox in that it is non-verbal and inflective: it articulates a non-articulable question which by its expressive force appears as an answer constituting itself as a question. 

The melody, perhaps, expresses a resolution of the nonverbal. It is that which expresses meaning without necessary recourse to the spoken word, superseding the paradox in more cohering fashion than the scream. I will speculate as an aside the possibility that the scream also implicates a type of rupture: it transgresses barriers of normative human action, implies either emergency, grief or joy, and signifies an extreme intensification, amplification or acceleration.

As it is the one who is of the aspect of Moses who engages the paradox by way of its embodiment, articulating without articulation, what occurs here is an act of Tikkun. This is the inflection of the inarticulable, a profanation in Agamben’s sense, through which that which cannot be overcome is enacted and thereby exceeded infinitesimally. 

This is the practice of implication of the Other, the unnameable, the question, the contradiction, into the self as an act of profanation of language through which emerges the reconstitution of the question: the nonverbal potentiality of motion into a hitherto impossible position or state.

The scream brings one past the darkness, through the void to the presence of God as creator by giving birth to a new state of consciousness, moving from the wordless to the word.
