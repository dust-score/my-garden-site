Israel ben Eliezer, known as the Baal Shem Tov or BeShT (c. 1700-1760, Ukraine), was a Jewish mystic and healer regarded as the founder of Hasidic Judaism. A _baal shem tov_ is a “Master of the Good Name”—that is, one able to work miracles using a secret name of God. In the famous epistle describing his ascent of the soul, the Besht asks the Messiah when he will come and receives the answer: when the Besht’s teachings are spread and others can perform the same unifications and ascents.

And I asked the Messiah: When do you come? And he answered: ‘You will know [the time], which is when your doctrine will be revealed in public and it will be disclosed to the world, and your fountains will well outside, what I have taught you and you apprehended, and also they [the people of Israel] will be able to perform the unifications and the ascents [of the soul] as you do, and then the shells will be abolished and there will be a time of good will and redemption.’

This idea mirrors the idea of Tikkun as the work of _small adjustment_. Although not a specifically Hasidic thought, (attributed rather loosely to “a real cabalist,”) this idea, circulated by Scholem among Benjamin and Bloch, and favoured by Adorno, who picked it up in _Aesthetic Theory_, describes the Eschaton as not necessarily hugely different from our own world. By extension, the work of Tikkun, “to establish the reign of peace[,] it is not necessary to destroy everything nor to begin a completely new world. It is sufficient to displace this cup or this bush or this stone just a little, and thus everything”. The eschaton therefore consists not in a dramatic shift in history but in a change in human nature.

R. Menahem Nahum of Chernobyl (1730-1797) was a student of the BeShT and a major figure in early Hasidism. In his work, he identifies the unification of speech and thought as the very “aspect of the Messiah,” attainable through daily prayer and Torah study:

This unification [of speech and thought] is a secret of the construction of the stature of the Messiah…  

It is certain that in every appropriate prayer, which includes the unification of thought and speech, the restoration is made, which is ‘an aspect of the Mashiyah.’  

Idel notes that “the inducing of the messianic repair by means of ordinary ritual activity, when performed with great intensity, obviously calls into question Scholem’s vision of the effect of messianism… As an individual, someone can complete his part in the messianic enterprise and enjoy an extreme experience, designated by the phrase ‘an aspect of the Messiah,’ without the need to defer the highest accomplishment to a utopian future.”

Idel is explicit that Hasidism marks a shifting of the understanding of Tikkun away from the Lurianic focus on the supernal anthropos toward the human person. I will note purely for future contemplation that the nature of the perfect unification now becomes prominently “a synthesis between two or more human acts, not between a plurality of divine powers”.

In these Hasidic sources, tikkun becomes an individually accessible _techne_ as opposed to an externalised,  redemptive force.  

The Hasidic emphasis on experience should be  understood as a move from hope to experience… In the ecstatic and Hasidic treatments of messianism we may presuppose the availability of the messianic mode of existence which offers an immediate experience as much informed by hope as by real attempts to actualize the messianic potentiality of religious life.

Nahman of Bratzlav (1772-1810), the grandson of the Baal Shem Tov, was an especially provocative Hasidic thinker who regularly deviated from the doctrinal norm.

As opposed to Luzzato, who adopted what Magid calls a more acosmic stance—viewing the experience of God’s absence as an illusion to be overcome by means of devotion, which could culminate in an experience of communion with God—Nahman approached the engagement with faith despite the experience of its empirical contradiction repeatedly in his work. His sensitivity lay precisely in an understanding of the existential suffering implied in the pursuit of God despite the experience of his absence. As such, he represents a broader paradigm shift, (of which he was unaware,) in which the medieval sense of God’s _incomprehensibility_ gave way to the more modern experience of humanity’s _estrangement_ from God. This shift, in turn, reflected the contemporaneous reorientation of western modern thought from the cosmos to the individual.

_Simple faith_ is a non-dialectical mode of religious devotion intended for everyone except the most elevated spiritual leader (the _zaddik_ of the generation). It is characterized by a complete rejection of philosophical speculation and reason as a basis for worship, requiring instead that God be served with pure simplicity. This faith avoids any confrontation with the rationally irresolvable questions of heresy that arise from the divine void, a struggle reserved exclusively for the _zaddik_ who possesses a “dialectical faith.”

But know, if there is a great zadik, who is of the aspect of Moses, he specifically needs to study these heretical matters. And even though it is impossible to resolve them as mentioned, nonetheless, through his study that he studies there, he raises up from there several souls that fell and sank into this heresy. […] And therefore the zadik who is of the aspect of Moses, of the aspect of silence, can study these perplexing matters, which are of the aspect of silence as mentioned, and he specifically needs to study, in order to raise up the souls that fell there as mentioned.

This is a direct engagement with _paradoxical faith_: relating to paradox not through its denial but by way of a kind of phenomenological imitation, through which an _Aufhebung_ (sublation) of the paradox could be enacted.

The second type of faith requires interaction with the heretic, a knowledge of philosophy, and thus the constant struggle to overcome the heretical question. This second, dialectical type of faith brings forth suffering and anguish; it is the faith of the zaddik, for only he can truly overcome the absence of God and reach the experience of the transcendent God.  

In Nahman’s thought, the experience of paradox derived from two separate, albeit related, sources. The first is the realm of non-absolute paradox that is of the aspect of failure within finitude, which is of the breaking of the vessels. This is the experience of ineptitude, of the chasm between ideal and real and the limitations in bridging the complexity of reality through limited subjectivity, whether in scientific, moral, philosophical, or other thought. Paradox can here be read as related to finitude in that it is an experience of the latter.

The first challenge confronting the one who seeks divine wisdom is the heresy rooted in the finite creation, the product of the breaking of the cosmic vessels in Lurianic Kabbala (shvirat ha-kelim). In this heresy, as in the finite world, God exists, and by finding the divine element in the philosophical question one can resolve the crisis of faith.

 The difficulties, i.e., the contradictions belonging to the act of the breaking of the vessels, are not absolute contradictions. … For at the time of the breaking of the vessels, sparks of holiness and broken letters fell, and these letters of the breaking of the vessels still constitute the last residue of rationality ruling in the realm of the breaking of the vessels. Therefore, it is possible to determine that the difficulties formed from the encounter between religion and the sciences are not absolute and can be resolved through the rational residue of their place of formation.  

This, the first type of paradox, is to be resolved by the engagement with its terms, the world. It is the second type of paradox, which derives from a transcendent condition, which evidently cannot be faced through rational engagement or even words.

The question of the second heretic cannot be resolved rationally. … The void is not a lacuna between the two dimensions of God, but the possibility of the nonexistence of the transcendent God, which makes the immanent God an illusion.

As a consequence of _tsimtsum_, the Empty Space is, before the introduction of any bridging between the resultant finitude and the retracted infinitude, a condition of possibility or potential but not manifestation. The second type of paradox arises from this, the question of the _possibility_ of finite existence within infinitude, or of infinitude (divinity) _within_ finitude. It is in its historical context the theological paradox generated internally to religion around the question of pantheism, the existence or non-existence of God _within_ the universe. Hasidic doctrine did not generally conceive of creation and the world within the _continuation_ of tsimtsum; instead, tsimtsum had been a condition of creation which had been overcome. According to Magid, Nahman is the only Hasidic thinker to propose a “tarrying” within tsimtsum.  “What interested him was that the void may serve as a foundation for the emotional response to the perception of divine absence in human experience.” 

The non-existence of God within the world and His existence within it despite everything … is the desperate contradiction of the world’s structure. How is it possible that there is no God within the worlds and yet there is?  

It is the second type of paradox which Nahman, according to Magid in _Through the Void_ (1995), faces with the scream. Weiss, in “_The Empty Space: Sabbateanism and its Melodies_,” does not engage with Nahman’s scream. Here, the tzaddik, who is of the aspect of Moses, and therefore of silence, faces the ultimate “difficulty,” which is also of the aspect of silence—the unutterable absolute paradox—with their own silence.

There is [thus] a […] type of heresy, made up of wisdom, which is really no wisdom at all. The perplexities and questions of this heresy come from the void (halal). They have about them the quality of silence, because no intellect nor language can resolve them. Creation came about through the word… In language there is intellect… language defines all things… But in the void which surrounds all worlds and is completely empty, there is no language. Therefore the questions which arise, they are silent.  

In both the silent response and the scream, we see an _implication_ of the nature of the paradox. It is not held at a distance, to be studied or resolved intellectually. This is of course clearly impossible. It is also not put aside. Rather, it is folded into the self. It is worth noting that in both cases it is emphasised that the tzaddik is of the aspect of Moses. In Nahman’s framework, the tzaddik who is of the aspect of Moses carries messianic capacities. This is because he performs an action isomorphic to Moses’ ascent on Sinai, bridging the unbridgeable, bringing articulation back from the inarticulable.
